Tesseract: a collective archive of local experiences


Tesseract is a city-wide catalog that collectively visualizes the personal and intimate experiences of individuals in a neighborhood area. It can serve as a source of information for those who are looking for opportunities to explore the city or an inspiration for those who encounter it serendipitously.

Duration: 5 weeks
Team Members: Shruti Aditya Chowdhury, Rachel Ng 
Project Brief: To discover an opportunity where intelligent sensing, city-wide systems can add value in the way in which people connect with one another in their surroundings throughout the day.
My Role: Primary Research, Secondary Research, Research Synthesis, Concept Development, Visual Design, UX Flow, Physical Prototyping

Identifying Design Opportunities
& Problem Spaces

We began our project with 20 interviews of Pittsburgh inhabitants, from different ethnicities, socio-economic background and ages ranging from 25 – 65.   To understand the radius of activities that locals engage in daily or weekly basis, we asked each person to draw a map of Pittsburgh. Thesemaps tended to only include places and landmarks they encountered very frequently on a day-to-day basis, and very little else.

I definitely want to venture more into the other neighborhoods instead of just staying downtown all the time ... I heard Lawrenceville is great for photography ... I'd like to explore it more but I don't quite know where to begin.


While many expressed a desire to find out more about other neighborhoods, communities and activities, they found it hard to take the initiative to explore outside of their comfort zones simply because they were not familiar with other areas. This was largely due to a lack of connectedness and dearth of opportunities for serendipitous encounters.

According to Jan Gehl, a Danish urbanist, who studied relationship between inhabitants and space; visibility and proximity to sensory experiences attract people to engage in city activities.
Can we introduce a system that visually connects a neighborhood to another to encourage locals to take the initiative to explore beyond their everyday routine? 

Introducing Tesseract:
Visual Collective of Local Experiences

Tesseract resolves the lack of visibility by providing a set of views (wormholes) of Pittsburgh neighborhoods with tagged vignettes of locals' experiences (tokens) that can be accessed not only in-situ, but also remotely.

Wormholes & Tokens

A wormhole is an AR visualization of vignettes of local Pittsburgh experiences (tokens) superimposed on top of the view of the neighborhood they were created in. This contextually organized information helps users become more familiarized with unvisited areas of the city.

Access to Wormhole views

Wormholes can be accessed via two different platforms:
1. public on-site viewfinders located in public spaces all around the city
2. personal mobile devices

The first one affords serendipitous discovery of experiences, and the latter serves as a source of information that the users can use to plan explorations.

Experience Prototyping:
Exploring Wormholes on Different Platforms


Tesseract allows users to access wormhole via two platforms: mobile and on-site viewfinders.
Since the system incorporates tangible interactions beyond screens, our team believed it is important to rapidly prototype our proposed experience and test it out. 

1. Viewfinder interaction (Knob Manipulation)

To investigate the most intuitive and natural way of interacting with the knob, our team built a non-working prototype (real scale) and took it outside for on-site testing.

Because of the cyclical and temporal nature of the features, we have decided to use a knob to perform the following functionalities on the physical viewfinder:


- generate new set of tokens

- navigate through tokens

- save tokens to users' maps

- see time-lapse of the view (play to see)

2. Mobile device interaction (Mapping the view to user's movement)

We also built a Wizard-of-Oz prototype to mimic the experience of accessing Wormholes on your mobile device. We interviewed a couple of people to see how they reacted to the concept, use of technology and interaction technique.
