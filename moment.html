Moment: Informing Drivers at Interruptible Moments


Driving is a task that takes up high cognitive load.
Often times, drivers experience difficulty taking in information other than navigational cues simply because they can't pay attention to them. Then, what if the information is presented when the cognitive load of the driver is low?

Duration: 3 months
Research Advisor: Seungjun Kim, Anind Dey 
Project Brief: To design intelligent information systems that enable drivers to naturally and effectively receive information in cars.
My Role: Speech Interaction Design, Literature Review, UI Design, Video Sketching

Designing a Better Way to Deliver Information to Drivers:
Use of Three Senses and the Right Timing

Assisting Dr.Seungjun Kim at Ubiquitous Computing Lab, I designed and simulated an intelligent in-car navigation system that informs drivers at interruptible moments. The team formulated two hypotheses about the elements that would affect the effectiveness of information delivery.


Hypothesis 1:
If information is presented at moments when the driver's cognitive load is low, it would be easier for the driver to absorb the information.


Hypothesis 2:
More the number of feedback methods, the more likely it is to capture the driver's attention.




Solution:
Present all visual, auditory, and haptic feedbacks to the driver at interruptible moments.

Thinking about Different Situations:
When to present information Briefly vs. in Detail?

The types of information are not limited to the directions but also include contextual and non-contextual notifications that the driver might find useful. These information pieces need to be presented appropriately depending on the length of the interruptible moment.

For short interruptible moments, 1 - 2 information pieces would be presented in brief audio descriptions. (ex) "Road construction ahead."

For long interruptible moments, 2 - 3 information pieces would be presented in detailed descriptions. (ex) "Road closed due to road construction, so expect 10 minute delay."
